# Concept Development Workflow

Use this reference when the task is to brainstorm, refine, critique, or document a game concept.

## Fast Path

1. Define the experience target.
   - What should the player feel, want, learn, or master?
   - What fantasy or role are they inhabiting?
   - What type of player is this for?

2. Name the core loop.
   - What does the player repeatedly do?
   - What feedback proves the action mattered?
   - What changes before the next loop?

3. Identify the design space.
   - List the simple variables that can interact.
   - Prefer many understandable variables over a few opaque rules.
   - Check whether the idea can generate content without adding constant new exceptions.

4. Map progression.
   - What opens up first?
   - What stays hidden until the player understands the base loop?
   - How can challenge scale without blocking all forward motion?

5. Define the hook.
   - What is legible in a screenshot, GIF, trailer beat, or 30-second explanation?
   - What is the surprising interaction that makes the game feel different?

6. Test the concept.
   - Write the smallest playable or paper prototype.
   - State one hypothesis before testing.
   - Observe player behavior before interpreting comments.

## Brainstorming Modes

- Divergent: generate many mechanics, fantasies, themes, constraints, and reference-game blends.
- Convergent: rank by hook clarity, design space, scope, novelty, audience, and prototype speed.
- Transformative: change one assumption: input method, resource, failure state, scoring, viewpoint, player role, time model, or social context.
- Constraint-first: design with a hard limit such as one button, one screen, 10 minutes per run, one resource, or no text.
- Reference inversion: take a reference game and invert what it optimizes, hides, rewards, or automates.

## Critique Checklist

- Experience: Does the mechanic actually produce the intended feeling?
- Loop: Is the repeated action tight, readable, and low friction?
- Consequence: Can the player see how choices changed the state?
- Depth: Are there enough simple variables for interactions to emerge?
- Accessibility: Is essential information conveyed through more than one channel when needed?
- Progression: Does the game teach through play without frontloading too much?
- Scope: Can the strongest version be prototyped quickly?
- Identity: Can the concept be recognized without explaining every rule?

## Output Biases

- For early concepts, produce sketches, lists, and decision points instead of fake certainty.
- For stronger concepts, produce a concise concept brief with core loop, design pillars, progression, content space, risks, and next prototype.
- Avoid implementation details unless they constrain the design.
- Preserve unusual ideas long enough to test them; do not sand them into generic genre templates too early.
