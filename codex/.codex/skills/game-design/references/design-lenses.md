## MDA

Mechanics are rules, data, controls, and content. Dynamics are the runtime behavior that emerges when mechanics interact with player input. Aesthetics are the desired player responses.

Use MDA backward for ideation:

1. Pick the target experience.
2. Describe the dynamics that would create it.
3. Choose mechanics that reliably produce those dynamics.
4. Playtest whether the player actually feels the target.

Common aesthetic targets include sensation, fantasy, narrative, challenge, fellowship, discovery, expression, and submission.

## Intention, Consequence, Story

Use Doug Church's FADT lens to test whether the player can form a plan, act on it, and see an outcome.

- Intention: The player can make a meaningful plan.
- Perceivable consequence: The player can see how the action changed the game.
- Story: Events bind together into a designer-driven or player-owned thread.

Strong designs do not maximize every tool everywhere. They choose where the player owns agency and where authored structure creates moments.

## Core Loops, Skill Atoms, and Mastery

Use loops when the idea feels vague.

- Action: What does the player do?
- Feedback: What teaches them what happened?
- Change: What resource, state, mastery, or possibility shifts?
- Return: Why do they want to repeat the loop?

Skill atoms are useful when teaching and progression are unclear: each new action should have a cue, an action, feedback, and a way for the player to improve.

## Player Motivation

Use motivation models as prompts, not as rigid personas.

- Self-determination theory: autonomy, competence, and relatedness.
- Quantic Foundry-style audience thinking: players differ in how much they seek action, social play, mastery, achievement, immersion, and creativity.

Check whether the concept has a clear primary audience and whether added features support or dilute that audience.

## Accessibility as Design Surface

Accessibility is not only a settings checklist. It affects core design clarity.

Ask early:

- What information is essential?
- Is essential information only visual, only auditory, or too time-limited?
- Can input be remapped, simplified, paused, slowed, or tuned?
- Can difficulty expose variables without breaking the core mechanic?
- Are subtitles, contrast, text size, motion, volume categories, and objective reminders considered?

## Playtesting

Treat playtesting as hypothesis testing.

- Decide what question the session answers.
- Watch behavior before asking opinions.
- Keep the test small enough to iterate.
- Separate critique playtests from public/blind playtests.
- Translate feedback into design problems before choosing fixes.

## User Preference Lenses

The user's design preferences currently emphasize:

- Tight loops with minimal friction between phases.
- Simple concepts that create large design space through interacting variables.
- High juice and polished feedback on every important action.
- Gradual progression and difficulty that let players choose pace.
- Readable synergies, visible trigger order, and clear combat/simulation output.
- Multiple layers of skill expression without forcing one best build.
- Rapid documentation, prototyping, and playtesting.
- Personal fun and distinctive systems over generic genre compliance.
